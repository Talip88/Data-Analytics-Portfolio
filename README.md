These screenshots are a set of business-intelligence dashboards about sales, inventory, marketing performance and profitability.
Very briefly, page by page:

1. Session Source / Medium (GA4 Sessions)

What it shows:

Total sessions, users, engaged sessions and overall engagement rate.

A donut chart of sessions by source/medium (Facebook paid, Google paid, Bing/organic, direct, Instagram paid, etc.).

Sessions by state to see which U.S. states bring the most traffic.

A funnel for User Acquisition (sessions → engaged sessions → add-to-carts → purchases).

A table by state with: sessions, users, engaged sessions, add to carts, checkouts and engagement rate.

Purpose:

Understand which channels and states are driving quality traffic and where users actually add to cart and purchase, not only visit.

2. Google Analytics / Product (GA4 – Product Ads & Revenue)

What it shows:

Total products viewed, added to cart, checked out and purchased, and total GA revenue.

Top 10 brands by revenue and top 10 states by revenue.

A detailed table per product (brand, SKU, product name) with views, add-to-carts, checkouts, purchases and revenue.

Highlight cards such as Top Viewed, Top Selling and High Demand Product SKUs.

Purpose:

See which products, brands and states generate the most revenue and how well each product converts from view → cart → purchase.

3. Bing Ads Report (Microsoft Bing Dashboard)

What it shows:

Total cost, clicks, impressions, CTR and ROAS for Bing Ads.

Charts by product for:

Cost by products

Impressions by products

CTR by products

A table by brand and SKU with revenue, cost, clicks, impressions, CTR, CPC, ROAS and POAS.

Purpose:

Evaluate performance of Bing campaigns at product level: which SKUs are efficient (good ROAS/POAS) and which ones spend but don’t convert.

4. Stock by Category (Inventory Category Focus)

What it shows:

Inventory in stock vs sold broken down by:

Super category (Cooking Equipment, Refrigeration Equipment, Beverage Equipment, etc.),

Second-level category (Back Bar Coolers, Baking Pans, Bar Supplies, etc.),

Third-level category (very detailed product types).

Tables listing categories with “In Stock” and “Sold” quantities.

Purpose:

See where your inventory sits and what actually moves, across all category levels. Helps with assortment planning and re-ordering.

5. Inventory Dashboard

What it shows:

High-level KPIs: total units in stock, inventory value, units sold, and revenue.

Stock breakdown by:

Status (available, presold, expected, returns, Walmart, etc.),

Vendor,

Warehouse/location (e.g. Murfreesboro vs Nashville).

Detailed table per item (warehouse, category, vendor, brand, SKU, item ID, entry date, sold date, status).

Purpose:

Monitor operational inventory health: how much stock is tied up, which vendors/warehouses carry most value, and which items are presold or slow-moving.

6. Sales Performance by State

What it shows:

Revenue KPIs plus number of projects and completed projects.

Revenue by month (with comparison to same period last year).

U.S. map: revenue by state.

Table by state and SKU with purchases and revenue.

Purpose:

Understand where you sell (which states) and how revenue is trending over time and by product.

7. Ads Performance by State

What it shows:

Total revenue from ads, number of projects, total ad cost, cost per click.

ROAS by state (bar chart and U.S. map) and ROAS by category.

Table by state with cost, sales, # of projects, profit, CTR, CPC, conversions, POAS and ROAS.

Purpose:

See which states and categories give the best advertising return and where ad spend is inefficient or unprofitable.

8. Closing Rate Dashboard

What it shows:

High-level performance: revenue, profit, sold products, total/complete/incomplete projects, cancellation rate, markup and overall closing rate.

Chart of closing rate by month and closed vs unclosed project revenue.

Closing rate by category, by marketing category, by salesperson, and by state.

Purpose:

Measure sales effectiveness: how well leads/projects are being closed, and who/where is performing best or worst.

9. Sales – Marketing Category

What it shows:

Global KPIs: revenue, profit, sold products, total projects, completion, cancellation rate, markup, closing rate.

For each month, stacked columns of:

Revenue by marketing channel (Call Rail, Web, Local, Account customers, Forms, eBay, Live Chat, Bid, Other).

Profit by marketing channel for the same months.

Purpose:

Compare marketing channels over time – which channels consistently drive revenue and profit, and in which months.

10. Location with Category

What it shows:

KPIs for a selected period: revenue, profit, sold products, projects, markup, closing rate.

U.S. map colored by markup.

Bar chart of markup by state.

Table by super category with revenue, profit, sold units, projects, closing rate, cancellation rate and markup.

Purpose:

Combine geography and product mix: which categories perform best in which regions, and where you have strong or weak margins.

11. Sales – Vendor

What it shows:

Overall KPIs: revenue, profit, sold products, total projects, incomplete projects, cancellation rate, markup, closing rate.

For each vendor:

Revenue vs same period last year

Profit vs same period last year

Sold products vs last year

Projects vs last year

Markup vs last year

Also “growth rate” charts for revenue, profit, sold products, projects and markup by vendor.

Purpose:

Manage brand/vendor portfolio: who is growing, who is shrinking, and where margin is improving or deteriorating.

12. Sales – Summary (Global Overview)

What it shows:

Very high-level totals: revenue, profit, sold products, total projects, completed/incompleted projects, cancellation rate, markup, closing rate.

Main chart: unclosed vs closed projects (revenue and profit) over time.

Breakdowns for:

Unclosed vs closed projects by marketing category

Revenue by vendor

Revenue by state

Purpose:

Provide management with a full snapshot of business performance and the main drivers by channel, vendor and state.
